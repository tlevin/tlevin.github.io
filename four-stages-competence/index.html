Four stages of competence in programming

10/01/2015
Learn To Code JavaScript

I started learning how to code in JavaScript in early 2015 and I had no direction, instruction, or plan. In fact I didn't even know that I wanted to learn JavaScript, I just heard of this "coding" thing on the internet.

This is not how to learn something technical.

Unconscious Competence

If you look at the four stages of competence model, you will start with unconscious incompetence. You don't know a skill and don't recognize that the skill is valuable. You can be oblivious to the entire tech industry and what a programmer actually does. I spent much of my life like this. Ignorance is bliss.

Conscious Incompetence

Once you gain the understanding that you don't know a skill and you think it is very valuable to learn, you start the process of learning. You have now entered one of the most frustrating stages, conscious incompetence. You want to learn, you just don't have the skills. You stumble and make mistakes, but this is the basis of learning.

Here is where most people form their base knowledge of a skill. You could scour the online resources such as codeschool, codecademy, treehouse, etc. Finding out what you don't know is important to building a plan. Get involved in discussions on Quora or Stack Overflow to build on your knowledge.

Conscious Competence

Conscious competence is the stage that people strive for; a point that with conscious thought you have knowledge of a skill. I think of it as having enough knowledge to be 'dangerous'. You can function professionally as a programmer at this stage, but you lack a certain level of 'optimization'.

Unconscious Competence

Unconscious competence is the final stage of mastery in a skill. You can code without significant concentration and thought. This is akin to 'autopilot' with coding. It is more often achieved through practice and persistence than any one learning resource.

In software engineering mastery of individual skills may be possible, but as a whole it is a constant learning process. That is why I love it.
